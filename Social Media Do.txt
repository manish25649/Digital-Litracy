# Social Media Do's and Don'ts for College Students

### Do's:
1. **Curate a Professional Image:** Use platforms like LinkedIn to highlight your academic projects, hackathon participations, and technical skills. 
2. **Review Privacy Settings:** Regularly audit who can see your posts and personal information on platforms like Instagram and Facebook.
3. **Think Before You Share:** Verify the credibility of news or information before retweeting or sharing it with your network.
4. **Engage Respectfully:** Participate in online discussions constructively, even when you disagree with someone's viewpoint.
5. **Google Yourself:** Periodically search your own name to see what your public digital footprint looks like to future employers.

### Don'ts:
1. **Don't Overshare Personal Information:** Avoid posting your exact live location, daily routines, phone numbers, or boarding passes.
2. **Don't Vent About Professors or Peers:** Never use public forums to complain about your university, classes, or classmates. 
3. **Don't Post Illegal or Inappropriate Content:** Pictures involving underage drinking, drugs, or offensive gestures will harm your reputation.
4. **Don't Feed the Trolls:** Avoid getting dragged into heated, toxic arguments or flame wars in comment sections.
5. **Don't Rely on "Deleting":** Remember that the internet is forever; screenshots exist, and deleting a controversial post doesn't erase it from history.


A situation where poor digital communication caused a real problem.
A classic example of poor digital communication causing significant issues is the "Reply All" disaster. In a well-known hypothetical scenario, a student receives a mass email from the university administration regarding a controversial new campus policy. Frustrated, the student writes a highly unprofessional and sarcastic complaint, intending to send it only to their close friend. However, they accidentally click "Reply All," broadcasting their rant to thousands of students, faculty, and the university dean. This results in severe embarrassment, a formal disciplinary warning, and damage to their academic reputation.
This could have been easily avoided with a few simple habits. First, always double-check the "To" and "CC" fields before hitting send. Second, draft sensitive or emotional messages in a separate document first, without the recipient email addresses filled in, to prevent accidental sending. Finally, maintain a professional tone on university networks, assuming any email could potentially be read by others.
